Access Code Program Director

September 2014 - June 2016

I worked with the team at Coalition for Queens (C4Q) to build and launch the Access Code program: a free 9-month intensive developer training program for adults from diverse and low-income backgrounds. I joined the C4Q team in 2014 as the third full-time team member (in addition to the two co-founders), and saw the team grow to 18 over my two year tenure, directly managing a team of four.

My primary role with the team was to lead the design and execution of Access Code, building upon the successes of the pilot program. In my time with the team, we ran two cohorts (one iOS and one Android focused) and built the curricula from scratch, recruited and engaged a community of 100+ technical volunteers, and engaged community partners to receive 1200+ applications for the 64 student slots. Graduates from this program have been placed at top-tier companies like Jet, OKCupid, Capital One, Pinterest, Kickstarter, and more, and the average income has increased from $18,000 pre-program to $85,000 three-months post-program.

Outside of this role, I wore many hats at C4Q as we grew the team to support our programs. I participated in various initiatives spanning everything from grant writing and partnership development to organizational growth planning and the development of ops systems.

Mining 911 Calls in New York City: Temporal Patterns, Detection and Forecasting

September 2013 - August 2014

During my graduate work at the Center for Urban Science and Progress (CUSP), I worked with a team of CUSP colleagues and the New York City Police Department (NYPD) to develop a predictive model for NYPD crime calls to provide data driven recommendations for police resource allocation.

Working closely with NYPD officers, our team utilized historical 911 and 311 call data, weather and census data to perform temporal behavioral clustering, develop predictive models of call activity, and develop a model for anomalous event detection. Read more about our work here.

Science Education at the Princeton Plasma Physics Laboratory

June 2011 - August 2013

In my time at the Princeton Plasma Physics Laboratory, I worked with the Science Education department in a variety of capacities. I started with a focus on dusty plasma research, and increasingly took on the role of a program manager for education programs focused on increasing access to science education for underrepresented groups.

Some of the initiatives I led included an after-school program for inner-city students focused on experiential learning, and a one-day Young Women's Conference designed to highlight the breadth of careers and opportunities available in the sciences. I also ran internship and mentoring programs, teacher-training workshops, and a variety of events for the general public to increase interest in the sciences.

This first exposure to the lack of opportunity for underrepresented groups to break into science and technology sparked my passion and interest in tackling problems outside of the laboratory.

Dusty Plasma Research in Microgravity

September 2008 - June 2011

As an undergraduate, I took part in a few different research initiatives. The most exciting (and extensive) of these was the Dusty Plasma Experiment (DPX), in which I worked with a team of undergraduate colleagues and scientists at the Princeton Plasma Physics Laboratory to participate in a zero-g flight with NASA’s Reduced Gravity Education Flight Program.

This program awards undergraduates the opportunity to submit experiment proposals to be conducted aboard a reduced gravity flight (aka the “vomit comet”) at NASA’s Johnson Space Center. DPX was an experiment designed to observe the behavior of Dusty Plasmas when removing the overwhelming force of gravity on the system. For three years, we had the opportunity to conduct our experiment and collect data aboard the zero-g flight.
